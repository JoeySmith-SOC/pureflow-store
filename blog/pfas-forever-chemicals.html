PFAS "Forever Chemicals" in Tap Water: What You Need to Know

June 2025 · 6 min read

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — PFAS — have been detected in water supplies serving over 200 million Americans. They're called "forever chemicals" because they don't break down in the environment or in your body. Here's what the science says, and what you can do about it.

What Are PFAS?

PFAS is a family of over 12,000 synthetic chemicals used since the 1940s in non-stick cookware, firefighting foam, food packaging, stain-resistant fabrics, and hundreds of industrial applications. Their defining characteristic: an extremely strong carbon-fluorine bond that resists heat, water, and biological degradation.

They accumulate in soil and groundwater, migrate into municipal water systems, and bioaccumulate in human tissue over time. The EPA set enforceable limits for six PFAS compounds in drinking water in 2024 — the first federal regulation of its kind.

Key fact: The EPA's 2024 PFAS rule sets maximum contaminant levels as low as 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS — lower than previous health advisory levels and a signal of how seriously regulators now take the risk.

Health Risks Associated with PFAS Exposure

Long-term exposure to PFAS has been linked to a range of serious health outcomes in peer-reviewed research:

  • Increased risk of kidney and testicular cancer
  • Thyroid disease and hormone disruption
  • Elevated cholesterol levels
  • Immune system suppression (including reduced vaccine effectiveness)
  • Developmental effects in children and infants
  • Pregnancy complications including preeclampsia

Does Boiling Water Remove PFAS?

No — and this is a common misconception. Boiling water concentrates PFAS rather than removing them, because the water evaporates but the chemicals remain. Standard pitcher filters like Brita also do not remove PFAS effectively.

What Does Remove PFAS?

The most effective technologies for PFAS removal in home filtration are:

  • Reverse osmosis (RO) — The most effective method, removing 90–99% of PFAS compounds. The semi-permeable membrane physically blocks PFAS molecules from passing through.
  • Activated carbon (high-contact-time) — Effective for longer-chain PFAS, less so for shorter-chain variants. Best used as a pre-filter in RO systems.
  • Nanofiltration — Effective but primarily used in commercial/municipal systems.
Bottom line: A certified reverse osmosis system is the most reliable, cost-effective method for removing PFAS from household drinking water. NSF/ANSI 58 certification specifically covers RO systems and their performance claims.
